ABOUT -- MT-32 / SCC / ADLIB

MT-32 was not merely one option. It was the intended TFX sound.

It would be easy to tell a neat technological story here:

MT-32 first, then Sound Canvas, then more realistic instruments, more ROM, more polyphony, more standardization.

That story is broadly true for PC sound hardware. But it is not the right story for TFX.

For TFX, the Roland MT-32 version is the version the music was written for.

That matters more than whether another device has a larger sound bank, more polished samples, or better General MIDI compatibility. The MT-32's patch structure, metallic attacks, synthetic pads, unusual percussion, and slightly alien timbres are not accidental limitations around the music. They are part of the music.

The SCC / Sound Canvas version is interesting because it shows how the material was translated into the newer General MIDI world. It may sound fuller in places, and its instruments may be more conventionally recognisable. But it does not have the same authority.

The MT-32 version has the strange digital atmosphere that belongs to TFX: a future imagined before everything became cinematically realistic. It is a little artificial, a little cold, occasionally metallic, and much more distinctive for it.

The clear winner is MT-32.

Not because it is objectively the most advanced synthesizer ever made, but because it is where the composition, the arrangement, and the sound design line up.

It is the version where the soundtrack sounds like it knows exactly what game it belongs to.

Sound Canvas: technically newer, but not necessarily more right

The Roland SCC-1 and SC-55 family represent a different idea of PC music.

They brought General MIDI conventions, PCM instrument samples, more familiar orchestral colors, and a much more standardized sound world. Their sound banks were larger -- around 3 MB for the SC-55/SCC-1 class, covering hundreds of patches and drum sounds -- and their instruments could sound more immediately recognisable than the MT-32's deliberately synthetic voices.

But "newer" and "more standard" are not the same as "better for this soundtrack."

The SCC version of TFX is best understood as a translation.

It takes the musical structure of TFX and maps it into a more conventional General MIDI palette. Some things survive well. Some become smoother. Some become more obviously instrumental. But something is lost too: the particular tension between melody, synthetic texture, and slightly strange sound design that makes the MT-32 score feel native to the game.

The Sound Canvas is a fascinating alternate interpretation.

The MT-32 is TFX.

AdLib: not the definitive version, but the one I remember

And then there is AdLib.

The AdLib version is not the version that Barry Leitch was primarily writing toward. It cannot reproduce the full MT-32 arrangements, the layered patches, or the particular timbral logic of the original score.

But it is the version I remember.

That is a different kind of truth.

My childhood TFX experience was connected to AdLib / Sound Blaster FM music: a Yamaha OPL chip with a tiny number of voices, no large sample bank, and almost no room for luxury.

The original AdLib OPL2 chip had only nine melodic channels, built from eighteen small FM operators. In rhythm mode, some of those channels had to be repurposed for percussion. There was no sampled orchestra waiting in memory. No lush string section. No realistic piano. No studio-quality reverb. No huge sound design toolkit.

Just oscillators, envelopes, modulation, feedback, frequency ratios, and a few carefully chosen tricks.

At a technical level, FM synthesis is almost brutally minimal.

One oscillator creates a simple waveform. Another modulates it. The result produces extra harmonics. With the right ratios, levels, envelopes, and feedback, a few sine waves become something that our ears interpret as a bell, a bass, a metallic hit, an electric piano, a brass-like sound, a drum, or an alarm from the future.

With the wrong settings, they become electrical noise.

And that boundary is part of the charm.

AdLib music often sits close to that edge: a few mathematical oscillators trying very hard to become music. The result can be thin, sharp, synthetic, and strangely deep. It has an 8-bit-like emotional texture -- not because OPL music is literally an 8-bit audio stream, but because it carries the same sense of radical compression, directness, and vivid abstraction.

A few channels. A few envelopes. A few modulated waveforms.

And somehow: atmosphere.

The arrangement has to be clever because there is nowhere to hide. Every note competes for one of a handful of voices. Every drum hit costs resources. Every sustained chord makes the next melodic decision harder. The composer and programmer have to think in terms of scarcity.

That is why these soundtracks can feel so concentrated.

Modern music production can use a hundred tracks, gigabytes of samples, convolution reverbs, layered synths, and unlimited automation. AdLib had a small chip that could create beauty or noise depending on a few register values.

The soundtrack is therefore not merely compressed.

It is distilled.

A browser is now emulating a machine that was already emulating instruments

The strangest part of this project is the chain of abstractions.

The original TFX music was not stored as finished audio. It was stored as compact musical instructions: timing, note events, patch changes, volume, panning, pitch bends, and other control data.

Those instructions were designed to drive old hardware.

That hardware then tried to create instruments.

The MT-32 combined small PCM attacks with synthetic partials to create pads, brass, percussion, and imaginary instruments. The Sound Canvas used a compressed sample bank to approximate an orchestra. The AdLib chip used a handful of oscillators and modulation paths to persuade the ear that mathematics could be music.

And now, decades later, JavaScript is running in a browser, scheduling those reconstructed musical events, while WebAssembly emulates the old synthesizer hardware, which then recreates its approximation of instruments.

The chain is almost comically indirect:

1993 game data-> extracted music events-> MIDI-style sequencing-> JavaScript timing-> WebAssembly emulation-> emulated MT-32, Sound Canvas, or OPL chip-> synthesized sound-> modern headphones

A browser tab is now effectively hosting a miniature museum of old sound hardware.

And the remarkable thing is that this did not require a huge engineering team or months of bespoke low-level work. Modern emulators, browser audio, WebAssembly, and AI-assisted development made it possible to assemble this in roughly twenty hours.

That is the science-fiction part.

But it is not the reason the project matters.

The technical work is only the path back

All of this can sound extremely geeky.

Custom data formats. MIDI events. OPL operators. LA synthesis. ISA cards. PCM ROMs. General MIDI mappings. WebAssembly. Browser scheduling. Synthesizer state restoration.

And yes, it is geeky.

But the point is much simpler.

As a child, I would sometimes leave TFX on the intro screen, or sit after a landing, or delay the next click in a menu, simply because I wanted the music to continue.

I was not analyzing MIDI channels.

I was not thinking about FM operators or waveform ROM sizes.

I just liked the feeling.

The MT-32 version now shows me what the soundtrack was truly designed to be: the rich, synthetic, slightly alien sound world that belongs to TFX itself.

The AdLib version gives me something else: the smaller, harsher, more compressed sound world that belongs to my own memory of playing it.

The jukebox holds both.

The technically "right" version, and the emotionally right one.

-- Voyta
